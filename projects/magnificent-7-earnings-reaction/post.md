Following up on my last post about earnings days: everyone assumes topping estimates sends a stock up, but last year the Magnificent 7 did it almost every quarter, and it barely moved them.

They beat EPS estimates on 24 of their 27 reports. On 16 of those, the stock fell anyway.

A few that stood out:

- Amazon beat by about 25% two quarters in a row. The stock fell 8% after the first quarter, then rose 10% after the second.
- Tesla missed by 7% one quarter and rose, then topped estimates by 16% in another and fell.
- Microsoft came in above estimates all four quarters, and the stock still dropped after three of them.

The size of the surprise told you almost nothing about the move: the correlation between them was just 0.2, very weak. What moves these stocks is guidance and how the numbers land against expectations that are already sky-high. A result that's priced in does nothing.

Topping estimates isn't the edge people assume. Beating expectations is.

Methodology: consensus EPS estimate vs reported (CoinCodex and company filings). Reaction = the next-day move (Yahoo Finance, split/dividend-adjusted), the last four quarters. Four reports had one-time tax or investment items, so I used operating EPS for three and excluded Alphabet's April report (a one-time $37B gain made its headline EPS meaningless).
