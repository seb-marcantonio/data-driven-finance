How much does a great World Cup actually add to your market value?

I pulled the Transfermarkt numbers from the last three tournaments, before the World Cup compared to after. It works like earnings season. Four weeks of performance, and the market re-prices you on the spot.

For individuals, the re-rating is steep:

• Azzedine Ounahi went from €3.5m to €15m at Qatar 2022, up 329% in weeks, then moved to Marseille.
• James Rodríguez won the 2014 Golden Boot, €35m to €60m, then Real Madrid paid around €75m.
• Enzo Fernández left the 2022 final a world champion, €35m to €55m, then Chelsea paid €121m, a British record.

And the market value bump is only the signal. The actual transfer usually clears well above it.

Zoom out to whole squads and the effect is real but much smaller. A national team is a portfolio of 20-plus assets, so a deep run only re-rates a few while the expensive core is already priced in.

The biggest gains went to the teams that beat expectations, not the favourites. Morocco's run to the 2022 semis lifted their squad about 16 percent, more than champions Argentina. The pre-priced elites moved least, with Belgium and England under 5 percent and France's 2022 squad around 6.

So how much does a good World Cup boost your value? If you are an unknown who breaks out, it can multiply it. If you are already a star, it nudges it. And for a whole team, the market pays for surprise, not the trophy.

It is happening again in 2026, with Spain's teenage winners Lamine Yamal and Pau Cubarsí and Morocco's Ayyoub Bouaddi. The official re-rate lands this week.
