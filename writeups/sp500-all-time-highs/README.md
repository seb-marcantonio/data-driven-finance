# Is Buying the S&P 500 at an All-Time High a Mistake?

This analysis tests the common fear that buying the S&P 500 at a record high is dangerous. Using Robert Shiller's long-run monthly total-return data, it shows record highs are normal (131 of the 360 months since 1996), and that forward returns after buying at a high slightly beat buying below the prior peak at one and five years and are about even at ten. It then pits dollar-cost averaging against timing strategies: DCA beat a buy-only-at-new-highs rule 100% of the time and a buy-on-10%-dips rule 86% of the time, and even perfect hindsight timing beat DCA by less than 1% a year. The angle: "time in the market beat timing it," with every number re-derived from the raw data across multiple verification passes.

## The LinkedIn post

Is buying the S&P 500 at an all-time high a mistake?

People assume that buying the S&P 500 at an all-time high is dangerous, like buying right before the drop. But the index has set hundreds of record highs, so I tested whether buying at highs actually hurts your returns.

First, record highs are normal, not warning signs. The index has set a new record high in 131 of the 360 months since 1996, including 18 of them in 2024 and 2025 alone.

Then I compared what you earned, depending on whether you bought at a record high or when the market was below its prior peak:
- One year later: +12.5% on average if you bought at a high, versus +11.7% if you bought below the peak.
- Five years later: +78% versus +72%. Ten years out, about even.
- Returns were positive about as often either way.

Buying at a high was not a disadvantage.

I also tested dollar-cost averaging, putting the same amount in every month no matter what the market did:
- Dollar-cost averaging beat a "buy only at new highs" strategy 100% of the time.
- It beat a "buy only on 10% dips" strategy 86% of the time.
- Even perfect, impossible timing (buying every bottom with hindsight) beat dollar-cost averaging by less than 1% a year.

Waiting in cash, for highs or for dips, usually costs money. Time in the market beat timing it.

Knowing this, would you buy at an all-time high?

Method: monthly S&P 500 total return (Robert Shiller data), dividends reinvested, nominal dollars. The return and strategy tests run from 1928 to 2025. Idle cash earns Treasury bills. The "buy at the low" case uses perfect hindsight and cannot be done in real time. This is analysis, not advice, and past performance does not guarantee future results.
